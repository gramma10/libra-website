Per-site evidence
Top 5
1. Fresha — fresha.com
Hero (consumer face): "Book local selfcare services" — sub: "Discover top-rated salons, barbers, medspas... trusted by millions worldwide." Scale signals: 130,000+ partner businesses, 450,000+ professionals, 1B+ appointments, 120+ countries. Capterra 4.8 (1,443 reviews), G2 4.8. Free for businesses (commission on marketplace bookings). Modern gradient aesthetic, mobile-first, lifestyle photography for testimonials. Wins on raw scale; loses a fraction on conversion because the main domain leads with consumer-side messaging rather than B2B.

2. GlossGenius — glossgenius.com
Hero: "Loved by 100,000+ Businesses" — sub: "Scheduling, payments, and admin. Done for you." Dual CTA: "Get started" (solo) / "Get a demo" (teams). Capterra 4.8, G2 4.4. Widely recognized as the best-designed product in the space — sleek lifestyle photography, beautiful POS hardware, mobile-first. The design lesson: it's the only one that puts social proof in the headline rather than below it.

3. Booksy — biz.booksy.com
Hero: "The best booking app to build your dream business" — sub: "Booking & Business Management Software Built for Growth." CTA: "Start free now." Scale: 310,000 beauty pros, 44M+ customers. Capterra 4.6 (319). Pricing transparent on page ($29.99/mo + $20/extra user). Bright, card-based modern layout. Hard-numbers-everywhere approach: "20% more bookings," "25% drop in no-shows."

4. Boulevard — joinblvd.com
Hero: "The tech behind your touch" — sub: "Meet the fastest growing client experience platform, trusted by thousands of appointment-based self-care businesses nationwide." CTA: "Get a Demo." 40,000+ self-care professionals. Awards stack: Capterra Best Value 2025, G2 Best Value 2025, Software Advice Best Customer Support, Deloitte Tech Fast 500 2024. Premium positioning — beats GG on conceptual headline, ties on visual polish.

5. Mindbody — mindbodyonline.com
Hero: "Business management software" — sub: "More revenue. More clients. More growth." CTA: "Get a demo." 40,000+ businesses, 720M+ classes/appointments booked annually, 3M+ active app users. Logos: Orangetheory, Drybar, FIT, Arch Amenities. The single best testimonial in the cohort: "Mindbody helped us grow from about $150,000 a month, to nearly $600,000 in revenue per month." (Rejuv Medical). G2 3.6 / Capterra 4.0 — surprisingly mixed despite scale.

Bottom 5
10. Square Appointments — squareup.com/appointments
Hero: "Appointments — Simplify bookings and stay organized." CTA: "Get started." No salon-specific count, no salon testimonials, no industry-specific logos. Square brand polish exists but the page reads as one-of-many for a payments giant. Penalty: not niching when your competitors do.

11. Timely — gettimely.com
Hero: "Salon software to help grow your business." CTA: "Dive into Timely with our interactive demo." Page extraction was thin — content is sparse above the fold or heavily JS-rendered. No customer count, no headline testimonials surfaced. Likely solid product, weak marketing-page craft.

12. Acuity Scheduling — acuityscheduling.com
Hero: "Scheduling software to power exceptional client experiences." 250,000+ customers — but Acuity is horizontal (legal, pet care, automotive, education, beauty). No salon photography, no salon testimonials, no salon logos. Generic.

13. Meevo — meevo.com

Logo wall is dense but uncurated. 4.6 Google / 4.4 Capterra. 20× Best Salon Software (industry-publication awards) is real, but design feels late-2010s.

Hero: "SOLUTIONS FOR MODERN SALONS" (all caps) — sub: "All-In-One Salon Software — Increase revenue and elevate the client experience..." CTA: "Start My Free Trial" (this is good — direct action). No customer count. Capterra 4.4. The all-caps headline alone signals dated brand voice; the design supporting it is generic SaaS-template territory.

What the top 5 do that the bottom 5 don't
This is the principles section — what to actually steal for your consultancy site.

1. Hard numbers in or one line below the hero
Top 5: "Loved by 100,000+ Businesses" (GG), "450,000+ professionals" (Fresha), "310,000 beauty pros" (Booksy), "Helping 40,000+ self-care professionals level up" (Boulevard), "More than 40,000 businesses" (Mindbody). Bottom 5: vague ("Loved & Trusted by Millions" — Meevo), generic ("250,000 customers" but not salon-specific — Acuity), or absent (DaySmart).
Steal it: lead with a specific count of businesses you've helped (or revenue/leads you've generated), measured in a way the prospect can verify or imagine themselves inside.

2. One confident promise, not a feature list
"The tech behind your touch" (Boulevard). "More revenue. More clients. More growth." (Mindbody). "Done for you" (GG). Bottom 5 cram the headline with what the product is — "Meevo For Salons, Spas, and Med Spas" reads like an HTML title tag. "SOLUTIONS FOR MODERN SALONS" in all caps is shouting without saying anything.
Steal it: your hero should make a transformation promise (what their business becomes), not describe what you sell.

3. Real people, not stock or product UI
Top 5 use lifestyle photography of actual salon owners, clients in chairs, hands at work. GG and Boulevard especially. Bottom 5 lean on product screenshots, generic stock, or logo walls without context.
Steal it: invest in a single photoshoot of one or two real salon clients you build for. Their faces > any UI mockup.

4. A specific dollar testimonial near the top
"Mindbody helped us grow from about $150,000 a month, to nearly $600,000 in revenue per month" — Rejuv Medical. Phorest has a revenue calculator. GG has "see what a difference 30 days can make." Bottom 5 testimonials are vague feel-good ("everything's in one place" — DaySmart).
Steal it: one named client + one number ("[Salon X] booked 142 new clients in 90 days from this site") beats five soft endorsements.

5. One dominant CTA, not three
Top 5: clear primary CTA — "Get a demo" or "Start free." Even GG's two CTAs are segmented ("Get started" for solo, "Get a demo" for teams) so each visitor sees one. Meevo offers three competing actions in the hero region. Decision paralysis kills conversion.
Steal it: one CTA in the hero. Segment it later if you must.

6. Niche language even when product is broader
Fresha, Booksy, GG all serve more than salons but talk to salon owners first. Acuity is the cautionary case — serves everyone, talks to no one specifically, scores worst on relevance.
Steal it: if you're niching to beauty, the whole site should speak salon-owner — copy, photography, examples, testimonials.

7. Pricing transparency or a strong free signal
Booksy shows the price ($29.99/mo). Fresha is free for businesses. GG has a clear /pricing page. Bottom 5 hide everything behind "Get a demo." For a consultancy, this is strong-form vs weak-form: published starter price ("websites from $X") signals confidence and filters tire-kickers.

8. Above-the-fold has four things at once
Headline + sub + single CTA + a proof element (logo wall OR a count OR a Capterra/G2 badge). All five top sites pass this test. Bottom 5 typically have headline + sub + CTA only — proof is buried.

9. Design vocabulary that signals the decade
Top 5: gradients, soft shadows, generous whitespace, rounded geometric type, smooth scroll-triggered animation, lifestyle photography. Bottom 5: tight layouts, sharp geometric blocks, all-caps headers, dense logo carousels, 2014-era SaaS-template feel.
Steal it: if anything on your site could have shipped in 2017, redo it.

10. Awards/ratings shown as visual badges, not bragged about in copy
Boulevard's award stack is 4 small badges in a row — read in 2 seconds. Top 5 use Capterra/G2 badges as visual furniture. Meevo tells you it's "20× Best Salon Software" — which feels promotional rather than proven.

What I'd do with this for your consultancy site
If you build the consultancy site applying the principles above:

Hero formula: transformation promise + named-business count (or revenue moved) + single CTA + Capterra/G2 equivalent (Trustpilot/Google reviews).
One photoshoot with two real client salons. Use those images everywhere.
One headline ROI testimonial with a real number near the top of the page.
Pricing transparency — at minimum a "from $X" so prospects qualify themselves.
Salon-specific everything — every example, screenshot, case study should be a beauty business.
